Because students were already accessing most of what they needed in the course through a web browser, my primary focus during the transition was on the pedagogical elements, speficially how to do lectures and labs in the context of the new circumstances. This not only meant figuring out the technology but also figuring out how to connect and engage with students who were now around the world, in varying home circumstances.

One of the first things I did was create a survey to send to my students. The survey I sent was largely based on the survey designed by Dr.¬†Kristen Stephens-Martinez [ADD LINK]. The survey served two primary purposes:

  1. It helped me get in contact with the students. Since the course had 90 students, this was the most efficient way to try to get in contact with everyone. I set a deadline to fill out the survey and indvidually reached out to students who hadn‚Äôt completed the survey by the deadline.

  2. It helped me better understand the circumstances students would be in as they finished the semester. Some of the most important pieces of information were the time zone and student‚Äôs ability to engage in live lectures and labs. These were presented as mutliple choice questions with relatively generic answers about whether they were set up to participate through Zoom during the lecture and lab times (which were required to remain the same as the regular semester). I provided an option for students to elaborate on their answer. I also ended the survey with giving students the option to let me know any questions or concerns they had.

    Sending the survey was by far the most valuable thing I did during the transtiion, as it helped me better understand the circumstances students were in. As I was planning for the course, having this informaiton helped me be more realistic about what the class would look like in the new remote format.

Changes

Here are a few of the key changes I made with the transition to remote learning. You can read about all of the changes and policy updates I made in the Remote Learning Updates [ADD LINK] page.

Lectures: The biggest change to the semester was the way I conducted lectures. Since this was the main component of the class that relied on in-person interaction (lecture with active-learning activities sprinkled throughout), this was what needed the most attention as I made the transition to remote learning. I ultimately decided to split lectures into two components and create some version of a flipped classroom:

-Pre-recorded live lecture videos: These were essentially like the traditional lecture, where I recorded videos talking through the lecture slides. There were questions sprinkled throughout where in-class discussion/activites would‚Äôve gone [LINK TO AN EXAMPLE], so I asked students to pause the video and jot down their thoughts/responses to the actvity questions. The pre-recorded lectures were posted about two days in advance in a discussion thread in Sakai, LMS. Students were told to either write their questions down to ask during the ‚ÄúLive Lecture Session‚Äù or to reply to the thread with their questions if they couldn‚Äôt attend be live for the lecture. I checked the discussion thread before each class, so I could make sure to address the posted questions in the live lecture.

  • Live Lecture Session: This was time I met with the class on Zoom during the scheduled lecture time. I made an agenda for each live lecture [ ADD LINK], so students would know what to expect. I typically started with announcements and asking if there any questions that were posted in the discussion thread or that students posted in the Zoom chat or asked verbally after raising their hand. Some quesitons I answered right away; others I jotted down to come back to later since I knew there‚Äôd be a more natural place to answer them.

    After taking questions, we basically went through the remaining time walking through an analysis covering the topic. Students could use an Rmd file or rendered HTML to follow-along. I usually talked about the code and more practical elements, since students got the basic terminology and definitions from the pre-recorded lecture. During most live lectures, I randomly split students into breakout rooms to talk about the discussion questions from the videos.

    I popped into a few break out rooms during the discussions (students were told beforehand this would happen), and it was really great. A lot of students had their video on in the breakout rooms and it just made everything feel more energetic than in the larger setting. Even as students came back to the main room, a few would keep their video on. I randomly selected a breakout room to respond to the class.

    The live lecture sessions were recorded and uploaded into the LMS, so students who were unable to attend live could still access the content.

  • Final project: Students had just begun their final group project [ADD LINK] before the transition, so I gave them the option to continue with the same project in their same groups or do something else. They overwhelmingly decided to stay in their same groups (I think because they had been with these groups since the 3rd week of the semester and had learned to work together). The project stayed largely the same, with the exception of the final presentation. Rather than present in-class, each group recorded a video presentation and uploaded it in a discussion thread in Sakai. I gave groups the option to upload their slides with a written transcript if video wasn‚Äôt an viable option. Students were randomly assigned three presentations to write and post comments. I‚Äôll talk about this more in [SECTION NAME - THINGS TO KEEP WITH LINK], but in summary I really liked this format!

  • Midterm 2: I give two in-class paper midterms during the semester where students are allowed to bring one page of handwritten notes. Given the exams would be done remotely, I opted for an open-note, open-book exam. I tried to keep the format as similar to the first exam as possible, so students typed their responses in a Word document of the exam, saved it as a PDF and uploaded their resposes to the LMS. The exam was written to be taken within 75 minutes, but students had 2 hours to take the exam to provide flexibility for any computing issues.
